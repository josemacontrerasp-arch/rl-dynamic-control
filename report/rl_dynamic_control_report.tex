\begin{abstract}
\noindent
I present a reinforcement-learning framework for the hour-by-hour dynamic
operation of a cement-integrated CO\textsubscript{2}-to-methanol synthesis
plant (213~kt/yr, 270~MW PEM electrolyser) under volatile electricity
pricing. Three RL algorithms---Soft Actor-Critic (SAC), Proximal Policy
Optimisation (PPO), and tabular Q-learning---are benchmarked against
rule-based and constant-load baselines. SAC delivers approximately
\textbf{+490\% profit} versus constant full-load operation and generalises
zero-shot across electricity markets (GB~$\leftrightarrow$~NL) with $<3\%$
reward degradation. Extensions cover real ENTSO-E price data, a
multi-objective Pareto sweep (profit vs.\ CO\textsubscript{2} utilisation),
temporal-fold robustness testing, and a targeted fix for GPR surrogate
extrapolation. With corrected surrogates the extrapolation warning rate
drops from 96\% to 0\%, yielding trustworthy absolute profit figures while
preserving the strong relative rankings.
\end{abstract}

\vspace{0.3cm}

% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}

The parent EURECHA project designs a large-scale CO\textsubscript{2}-to-methanol
process co-located with a cement kiln. Fresh CO\textsubscript{2}
(899~kmol/hr) is hydrogenated with green H\textsubscript{2} from a 270~MW
PEM electrolyser. A recycle loop with purge achieves 99.7\% overall
conversion, producing $\sim$213~kt/yr of methanol at a levelised cost
(LCOM) of \pounds779--1303/t depending on the electricity price.

Steady-state optimisation via Gaussian Process (GP) surrogates and
$\varepsilon$-constraint Pareto sweeps is described in the parent project.
This sub-project adds \textbf{dynamic operation}: an RL agent modulates
the electrolyser load, reactor temperature, and reactor pressure each hour
to maximise profit under fluctuating day-ahead electricity prices.

The key question is: \emph{how much value does intelligent hourly dispatch
add over simply running the plant at full load?}

% ============================================================
\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Gymnasium Environment}

The plant is modelled as an hourly-step Gymnasium environment with an
8-dimensional observation space (electricity price, electrolyser load,
H\textsubscript{2} buffer level, reactor $T$, reactor $P$, methanol rate,
hour-of-day, day-of-week) and a 3-dimensional continuous action space
(load setpoint, $T$ setpoint, $P$ setpoint). First-order lag dynamics
($\tau_{\text{load}}=0.3$, $\tau_T=0.5$, $\tau_P=0.7$~hr) simulate
realistic plant inertia.

\subsection{Surrogates and Reward}

Plant outputs (H\textsubscript{2} production, methanol rate, energy
consumption) are predicted by GP surrogates trained on 500 Latin Hypercube
samples from the simplified process model calibrated to the Aspen Plus
base case (LCOM = \pounds938/t at $T=230\,{}^{\circ}\text{C}$,
$P=70\,\text{bar}$, $F_{\text{H}_2}=2704$~kmol/hr). The hourly reward is:
\begin{equation}
  R = \underbrace{\text{MeOH revenue} + \text{CO}_2\text{ credits}}_{\text{income}}
      - \underbrace{\text{electricity cost} + \text{utility cost}
      + \text{ramp penalty} + \text{constraint penalty}}_{\text{costs}}
\end{equation}
scaled by $10^{-3}$ for numerical stability. All economic parameters
(e-methanol \pounds800/t, CO\textsubscript{2} credits \pounds80/t,
electrolyser 50~kWh/kg~H\textsubscript{2}) are single-sourced from the
project constants module with no duplication.

\subsection{Agents}

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{SAC} (Soft Actor-Critic): continuous off-policy;
        $\gamma=0.99$, lr $=3\times10^{-4}$, buffer $10^5$,
        500k timesteps.
  \item \textbf{PPO} (Proximal Policy Optimisation): continuous on-policy;
        2048-step rollouts, 10 epochs, clip 0.2.
  \item \textbf{Q-learning}: tabular, $\varepsilon$-greedy on a
        $10\times5\times5=250$ discrete action grid.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Baselines}

Two baselines provide context: (1)~\emph{constant full-load} at nominal
$T/P$, and (2)~a \emph{price-threshold rule} that reduces load to 30\%
when the spot price exceeds \pounds65/MWh.

\section{Extension 1: Real ENTSO-E Prices \& Cross-Market Transfer}

The environment was extended to accept real Dutch day-ahead prices from
the ENTSO-E Transparency Platform (2023--2024, 17\,544 hours). All agents
were retrained on this NL data and evaluated in a $2\times3$ transfer
matrix: (GB-trained, NL-trained) $\times$ (SAC, PPO, Q-learning) evaluated

\section{Extension 2: Multi-Objective Pareto Sweep}

The reward was generalised to a scalarised bi-objective:
$R = \lambda_{\pi}\cdot R_{\text{profit}} + \lambda_c \cdot u_{\text{CO}_2}$,
where $u_{\text{CO}_2}$ is the per-step CO\textsubscript{2} utilisation
fraction. A sweep of $\lambda_\pi \in \{0, 0.125, \ldots, 1\}$ trains a
fresh SAC for each weight.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{figures/fig_pareto_front.png}
  \caption{Pareto front: profit vs.\ CO\textsubscript{2} utilisation.
           All SAC policies cluster at $\sim$93.5\% utilisation regardless
           of weight, revealing a \textbf{degenerate trade-off}. Baselines
           are shown as stars.}
  \label{fig:pareto}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Finding: the profit/utilisation Pareto is degenerate.}
CO\textsubscript{2} utilisation saturates at $\sim$93.5\% wherever the
plant runs profitably, because the reactor's high per-pass conversion
($\sim$63\%) and recycle loop make utilisation essentially insensitive to
the operating point within the feasible domain. A meaningful
multi-objective formulation for this plant would pit profit against
electricity consumption or ramping cost rather than CO\textsubscript{2}
utilisation.

\section{Extension 3: Robustness Testing}

Temporal-fold cross-validation tests whether the SAC policy is sensitive
to the specific price regime it was trained on. Six folds were constructed
from the NL dataset: two half-year swaps (H1$\leftrightarrow$H2) and four
leave-one-season-out (LOSO) folds. For each fold I compare: (i)~SAC
retrained from scratch on the fold's training split, (ii)~the original
GB-trained SAC evaluated zero-shot, and (iii)~the constant full-load baseline.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{figures/fig_robustness_bar.png}
  \caption{Robustness across temporal folds. The GB-trained SAC (orange,
           zero-shot) \textbf{beats the fold-retrained SAC} (teal) on
           every fold and has the tightest error bars.}
  \label{fig:robustness}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.55\textwidth]{figures/fig_robustness_heatmap.png}
  \caption{Leave-one-season-out heatmap (retrained SAC). Q4 (winter) is
           the hardest held-out season (reward 2328), Q3 (summer/autumn)
           the easiest (3198).}
  \label{fig:heatmap}
\end{figure}

The headline result is that \textbf{zero-shot transfer from the GB-trained
agent beats every fold-retrained agent on NL data} (range 3180--3287 vs.\
2328--3198). The full-year GB training set provides more diverse price
regimes than any 6- or 9-month fold. Winter quarters (Q1, Q4) are the
hardest for both the baseline and retrained models; the RL agent adds
the most value precisely when prices are highest---a 20$\times$ lift over
full-load in Q1.

\section{Extension 4: GPR Extrapolation Fix}

\subsection{Diagnosis}

The environment flags GPR extrapolation when the MeOH surrogate's
predictive $\sigma$ exceeds $2\times$ its training-set mean. The original
SAC triggers this warning on \textbf{96.1\%} of evaluation steps. The
agent has converged to an operating regime---low load ($\sim$0.05--0.15),
high $T$ ($\sim$275--280$^\circ$C), high $P$ ($\sim$85--100~bar)---that
the original 500-point LHS simply did not cover.

\subsection{Two Complementary Fixes}

\textbf{Approach A} retrains the GPR surrogates on an augmented dataset:
the original 500 LHS points plus 300 new points biased toward the agent's
operating region (pseudo-labelled by the simplified process model, not Aspen).

\textbf{Approach B} adds a variance penalty to the reward:
$R_{\text{safe}} = R_{\text{base}} - \alpha \cdot \max(0,\;\sigma_{\text{gpr}} - \sigma_{\text{threshold}})$,
discouraging the agent from high-uncertainty regions during training.

Three SAC variants isolate each fix:
(a)~v2 surrogates only,
(b)~v1 + variance penalty,
(c)~v2 + variance penalty.

\subsection{Interpretation}

The $\sim$10\% reward reduction (3194~$\to$~2916) reflects the surrogates
providing more conservative---and more honest---predictions in the agent's
preferred operating regime. The original reward was inflated by
extrapolation optimism. V2 variants also show substantially lower reward
variance (std $\sim$36--40 vs.\ 50--52), indicating more stable and
trustworthy policies. The CO\textsubscript{2} utilisation similarly
corrects downward from 93.5\% to $\sim$84\%, likely reflecting the
same artefact.

Critically, \textbf{all RL variants still massively outperform the
baselines} even after correction: variant~(c) delivers +487\% versus
the full-load baseline.

\section{Conclusions}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{SAC is the dominant algorithm}, delivering $\sim$+490\%
        profit versus constant full-load operation (corrected figure,
        0\% extrapolation).
  \item \textbf{Zero-shot cross-market transfer works.} A single policy
        trained on GB data generalises to the Dutch market with $<$3\%
        degradation---and outperforms fold-retrained models.
  \item \textbf{The profit/CO\textsubscript{2}-utilisation Pareto is
        degenerate}: utilisation saturates at $\sim$93.5\% across all
        profitable operating points, meaning a meaningful multi-objective
        study must use a different second objective.
  \item \textbf{Winter is where the RL agent earns its keep}: the full-load
        baseline nearly breaks even in Q1/Q4 while the SAC agent delivers
        20$\times$ higher reward---intelligent dispatch matters most when
        electricity is scarce and expensive.
  \item \textbf{GPR extrapolation was a real problem} (96\% of steps),
        but retraining on wider-domain data (Approach~A) completely
        eliminates it, with the combined v2+penalty variant delivering
        trustworthy, low-variance reward estimates.
  \item \textbf{Limitation}: v2 surrogates use pseudo-labels from the
        analytical model; running the 300 augmented points through
        Aspen Plus would yield a v3 surrogate with genuine bias
        correction.
\end{enumerate}
